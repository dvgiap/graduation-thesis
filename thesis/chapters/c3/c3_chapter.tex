\chapter{Methodology}
\label{ch:methodology}

This chapter presents the proposed methodology in formal terms. It first defines the underlying intrinsic-augmented decision process and the overall system architecture. It then specifies the intrinsic reward generator used in the implemented system, formulates the Correlation-Aware Reward Exploration (CARE) framework with its Beta Network and correlation-based objective, and concludes with the complete training algorithm. Five formal results (Propositions 3.1--3.3, Lemma 3.1, Corollary 3.1) are stated in this chapter and proved in Appendix A.

\section{System Architecture}
\label{sec:system_architecture}

The proposed framework is built on top of an intrinsic-augmented Markov decision process (MDP) and introduces a learned, state-dependent scaling function for intrinsic rewards. The two ingredients are made explicit in the following definitions.

\begin{definition}[Intrinsic-Augmented MDP]
\label{def:augmented_mdp}
An \emph{intrinsic-augmented MDP} is a tuple $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, R^E, R^I, \gamma, \rho_0)$ in which $(\mathcal{S}, \mathcal{A}, P, R^E, \gamma, \rho_0)$ defines a standard MDP with extrinsic reward function $R^E : \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A} \to \mathbb{R}$, and $R^I : \mathcal{S} \times \mathcal{A} \times \mathcal{S} \to \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$ is an additional, internally generated intrinsic reward function. The agent observes only $(s_t, a_t, R_t^E, s_{t+1})$ from the environment; the intrinsic reward $R_t^I$ is computed by the agent itself.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[State-Dependent Reward Shaping]
\label{def:reward_shaping}
A \emph{state-dependent reward shaping function} is a measurable map $\beta : \mathcal{S} \to \mathbb{R}_+$. Given an intrinsic-augmented MDP and a global intrinsic-strength hyperparameter $\alpha > 0$, the corresponding shaped reward at time step $t$ is
\begin{equation}
\bar{r}_t = R_t^E + \alpha\, \beta(s_t)\, R_t^I.
\label{eq:shaped_reward}
\end{equation}
The standard fixed-coefficient formulation corresponds to the special case $\beta(s) \equiv 1$.
\end{definition}

The proposed system instantiates this formulation with a parameterized $\beta_\psi$ and a specific intrinsic reward generator (ICM in the implemented experiments). The full pipeline is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:care_arch}.

The shaped reward used throughout this thesis follows directly from Definition~\ref{def:reward_shaping}. For the chosen intrinsic reward module $m$ with rectified intrinsic signal $I_t^{+,m}$ (Section~\ref{sec:icm}), the combined reward optimized by PPO is
\begin{equation}
\bar{r}_t \;=\; R_t^E \;+\; \alpha\, \beta_\psi(s_t)\, I_t^{+,m}.
\label{eq:combined_reward}
\end{equation}
Conceptually, $\beta_\psi(s_t)$ acts as a state-conditional volume control on curiosity: it amplifies intrinsic reward in states where exploration is empirically associated with future extrinsic success and attenuates it elsewhere. The remainder of this chapter formalizes this intuition.

\section{Intrinsic Reward Generation: ICM Instantiation}
\label{sec:icm}

In the implemented system, the intrinsic reward generator is the Intrinsic Curiosity Module (ICM) \cite{pathak2017curiosity}. Although CARE is module-agnostic, ICM provides a well-studied and computationally manageable case for quantitative evaluation.

\textbf{Forward and Inverse Dynamics Models.} ICM first encodes each state into a latent representation $\phi(s_t) \in \mathbb{R}^d$. It then trains two auxiliary models: a forward dynamics model $f^F_\eta$ that predicts the next latent feature from the current feature and action, and an inverse dynamics model $f^I_\eta$ that predicts the action from two consecutive latent states. The combined ICM loss is
\begin{equation}
L_\text{ICM}(\eta) \;=\;
\alpha_F \, \mathbb{E}\!\left[\tfrac{1}{2}\!\left\| f^F_\eta(\phi_t, a_t) - \phi_{t+1} \right\|_2^2\right]
\;+\; \alpha_I \, \mathbb{E}\!\left[ -\log p_\eta(a_t \mid \phi_t, \phi_{t+1}) \right],
\label{eq:icm_loss}
\end{equation}
with weighting coefficients $\alpha_F, \alpha_I > 0$.

\textbf{Raw Intrinsic Reward.} The raw intrinsic reward at time step $t$ is the forward prediction error
\begin{equation}
I_t \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left\| f^F_\eta(\phi_t, a_t) - \phi_{t+1} \right\|_2^2.
\label{eq:icm_raw}
\end{equation}
Because the magnitude of $I_t$ is not stationary across training, the raw signal is preprocessed before being passed to CARE.

\textbf{Standardization and Rectification.}

\begin{definition}[Standardized Intrinsic Reward]
\label{def:standardized_intrinsic}
Given a minibatch with empirical mean $\mu_I$ and standard deviation $\sigma_I$ of the raw intrinsic rewards, and a small constant $\varepsilon > 0$, the \emph{standardized intrinsic reward} is
\begin{equation}
I_t^+ \;=\; \max\!\left( 0,\; \frac{I_t - \mu_I}{\sigma_I + \varepsilon} \right).
\label{eq:icm_standardized}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

This preprocessing suppresses transitions whose prediction errors are below the batch average and retains relatively surprising transitions, which keeps the input to the Beta Network bounded in scale and approximately stationary across updates. In what follows, we use the generic notation $I_t^{+,m}$ to emphasize that the same construction applies to other intrinsic reward modules: replacing ICM with a count-based bonus or with Random Network Distillation (RND) requires changing the definition of $I_t$ but leaves the rest of the pipeline (standardization, Beta Network, correlation loss) unchanged.

\section{Correlation-Aware Reward Exploration}
\label{sec:care}

CARE introduces a state-dependent scaling function $\beta_\psi(s_t)$ between the intrinsic reward generator and the policy update. This section formalizes the network, its training objective, and the reasons for choosing a first-order optimization scheme over a meta-gradient one.

\subsection{Beta Network}
\label{sec:beta_network}

\begin{definition}[Beta Network]
\label{def:beta_network}
The \emph{Beta Network} is a parameterized map
\begin{equation}
\beta_\psi : \mathcal{S} \to [\beta_\text{min}, \beta_\text{max}], \qquad 0 < \beta_\text{min} < \beta_\text{max} < \infty,
\label{eq:beta_network}
\end{equation}
which assigns a positive, bounded scaling factor to each state. In the implemented system, $\beta_\psi$ is realized as a two-layer multilayer perceptron with embedding dimension 256, sigmoid output, and clipping to $[\beta_\text{min}, \beta_\text{max}] = [0.1, 2.0]$.
\end{definition}

For an intrinsic reward module $m$, the scaled intrinsic reward at time step $t$ is
\begin{equation}
\tilde I_t^{\,m} \;=\; \beta_\psi(s_t)\, I_t^{+,m},
\label{eq:scaled_intrinsic}
\end{equation}
and substituting Equation~\eqref{eq:scaled_intrinsic} into Equation~\eqref{eq:combined_reward} yields the shaped reward used by PPO.

The boundedness assumption built into Definition~\ref{def:beta_network} ensures that the shaped reward cannot grow arbitrarily large relative to the extrinsic signal, regardless of the behavior of the Beta Network during training.

\begin{lemma}[Bounded Shaped Reward]
\label{lem:bounded_shaped}
Let $\beta_\psi$ satisfy Definition~\ref{def:beta_network} and assume the standardized intrinsic reward is bounded almost surely, $|I_t^{+,m}| \le M$ for some constant $M < \infty$. Then for every time step $t$,
\begin{equation*}
\left| \bar{r}_t - R_t^E \right| \;\le\; \alpha\, \beta_\text{max}\, M.
\end{equation*}
Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:proof_bounded}.
\end{lemma}

Lemma~\ref{lem:bounded_shaped} is what allows CARE to be inserted into a PPO training loop without introducing additional instability: the shaped reward differs from the extrinsic reward by a bounded perturbation, so the policy gradient remains bounded under the same regularity conditions that PPO already requires.

\subsection{Correlation-Based Objective}
\label{sec:correlation_loss}

The Beta Network is trained so that the scaled intrinsic reward $\tilde I_t^{\,m}$ correlates with the discounted future extrinsic return. For a trajectory of length $T$, the discounted extrinsic return from time step $t$ is
\begin{equation}
G_t^E \;=\; \sum_{k=t}^{T-1} \gamma^{k-t}\, R_k^E.
\label{eq:extrinsic_return}
\end{equation}
Within a minibatch with empirical means $\mu_{\tilde I}, \mu_G$ and standard deviations $\sigma_{\tilde I}, \sigma_G$, both the scaled intrinsic reward and the extrinsic return are standardized:
\begin{equation}
\hat I_t^{\,m} \;=\; \frac{\tilde I_t^{\,m} - \mu_{\tilde I}}{\sigma_{\tilde I}}, \qquad
\hat G_t \;=\; \frac{G_t^E - \mu_G}{\sigma_G}.
\label{eq:standardization}
\end{equation}
The correlation-based loss is then
\begin{equation}
L_\text{corr}(\psi) \;=\; -\, \mathbb{E}\!\left[ \hat I_t^{\,m}\, \hat G_t \right],
\label{eq:corr_loss}
\end{equation}
and to prevent degenerate scaling a log-space regularizer pulls $\beta_\psi$ toward a reference value $\beta_0 = 1$:
\begin{equation}
L_\text{reg}(\psi) \;=\; \mathbb{E}\!\left[ \left( \log \beta_\psi(s_t) - \log \beta_0 \right)^2 \right].
\label{eq:reg_loss}
\end{equation}
The complete objective minimized at each update is
\begin{equation}
L_\beta(\psi) \;=\; L_\text{corr}(\psi) \;+\; \lambda_\text{reg}\, L_\text{reg}(\psi).
\label{eq:beta_loss}
\end{equation}

The correlation loss has a precise statistical interpretation, and its gradient has a precise sign condition.

\begin{proposition}[Pearson Equivalence]
\label{prop:pearson_equiv}
In the limit of an infinitely large minibatch, the empirical estimator of $L_\text{corr}(\psi)$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:corr_loss} converges to the negative population Pearson correlation between $\tilde I_t^{\,m}$ and $G_t^E$:
\begin{equation*}
L_\text{corr}(\psi) \;=\; -\,\rho\!\left( \tilde I_t^{\,m},\, G_t^E \right).
\end{equation*}
Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:proof_pearson}.
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:pearson_equiv} clarifies that minimizing $L_\text{corr}$ is not a heuristic surrogate but is equivalent (up to a sign) to maximizing the Pearson correlation between the scaled intrinsic reward and the future extrinsic return. The next result shows that the gradient with respect to $\beta_\psi$ at a state $s$ has a clean directional interpretation.

\begin{proposition}[Gradient Direction]
\label{prop:gradient_direction}
Assume the trajectories used to estimate $L_\text{corr}(\psi)$ are sufficiently diverse so that perturbations of $\beta_\psi$ at an individual state $s$ have a negligible effect on the batch statistics $\mu_{\tilde I}, \sigma_{\tilde I}$. Then for every state $s \in \mathcal{S}$ that is visited with positive probability,
\begin{equation*}
\frac{\partial L_\text{corr}}{\partial \beta_\psi(s)} \;<\; 0
\quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad
\mathbb{E}\!\left[ I_t^{+,m}\, G_t^E \,\big|\, s_t = s \right] \;>\; \mathbb{E}\!\left[ I_t^{+,m} \right]\, \mathbb{E}\!\left[ G_t^E \right].
\end{equation*}
Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:proof_gradient}.
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:gradient_direction} formalizes the central design intuition of CARE: a gradient step on the correlation loss pushes $\beta_\psi(s)$ upward exactly at those states where intrinsic reward and future extrinsic return are positively correlated, and downward elsewhere. In other words, the Beta Network is supervised by the empirical correlation pattern of the agent's own experience.

\subsection{First-Order Optimization vs. Meta-Gradient}
\label{sec:first_order}

A natural alternative to the correlation-based objective is to learn the intrinsic reward weighting via meta-gradients, as in Learning Intrinsic Rewards for Policy Gradient methods (LIRPG) \cite{zheng2018lirpg}. In that approach, the parameters of the intrinsic reward function are updated so that, after a policy update using the shaped reward, the resulting policy maximizes the extrinsic objective. This requires differentiating through one or more inner policy updates, which is computationally expensive and can be unstable in deep reinforcement learning settings.

CARE replaces the meta-gradient by a first-order objective that supervises $\beta_\psi$ directly using observed correlation. The cost difference is quantified by the following result.

\begin{proposition}[Computational Cost]
\label{prop:cost}
Per Beta-network update of size $T$, let $K$ denote the number of inner policy update steps used in a meta-gradient estimator, $|\theta|$ the number of policy parameters, and $|\psi|$ the number of Beta-network parameters. Then a meta-gradient training step has worst-case time complexity $O(K\, T\, |\theta|)$, while a CARE first-order training step has time complexity $O(T\, |\psi|)$. In particular, when $|\psi| \ll |\theta|$ and $K \ge 1$, the CARE update is asymptotically cheaper by a factor of $K\, |\theta| / |\psi|$.
Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:proof_cost}.
\end{proposition}

In the implemented system, $|\psi|$ is roughly two orders of magnitude smaller than $|\theta|$, so the cost reduction is substantial. The price of this efficiency is a weaker form of supervision: instead of optimizing $\beta_\psi$ directly for downstream extrinsic performance, CARE optimizes it for an empirical correlation that is a proxy for that performance. The next result describes the limit in which this proxy becomes uninformative.

\begin{corollary}[Degeneration to Fixed Coefficient]
\label{cor:degeneration}
Consider the objective $L_\beta(\psi)$ in Equation~\eqref{eq:beta_loss}. As $\lambda_\text{reg} \to \infty$, every minimizer $\psi^\star$ satisfies $\beta_{\psi^\star}(s) \to \beta_0 = 1$ uniformly in $s$, and the shaped reward in Equation~\eqref{eq:combined_reward} reduces to the fixed-coefficient form $\bar r_t = R_t^E + \alpha\, I_t^{+,m}$.
Proof in Appendix~\ref{app:proof_degeneration}.
\end{corollary}

Corollary~\ref{cor:degeneration} has both a theoretical and a practical reading. Theoretically, it shows that CARE strictly contains fixed-coefficient PPO+ICM as a limiting case, so it cannot perform worse than that baseline once $\lambda_\text{reg}$ is tuned appropriately. Practically, it explains an empirically observed phenomenon: when extrinsic rewards are extremely sparse, $L_\text{corr}$ provides little signal, the regularizer dominates, and CARE behaves like a fixed-coefficient method instead of degrading the policy.

\section{Algorithm}
\label{sec:algorithm}

This section formalizes the data structures, loss functions, and training procedure used to optimize the system end to end.

\subsection{Rollout Buffer}
\label{sec:rollout_buffer}

Each PPO update operates on an on-policy rollout buffer of length $T$ collected by the current policy. A single transition is the tuple
\begin{equation}
\xi_t \;=\; \bigl( s_t,\; a_t,\; \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t),\; R_t^E,\; s_{t+1},\; d_t \bigr),
\label{eq:transition_tuple}
\end{equation}
where $d_t \in \{0, 1\}$ is the episode termination flag. The buffer of length $T$ is stored as the tensors
\begin{equation*}
\mathcal{S}_\text{tensor} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_s}, \quad
\mathcal{A}_\text{tensor} \in \mathbb{N}^{T}, \quad
\mathcal{R}_\text{tensor} \in \mathbb{R}^{T}, \quad
\mathcal{S}'_\text{tensor} \in \mathbb{R}^{T \times d_s}, \quad
\mathcal{D}_\text{tensor} \in \{0,1\}^{T},
\end{equation*}
together with the log-probabilities $\mathcal{P}_\text{tensor} \in \mathbb{R}^{T}$. Following the implementation defaults, $T = 4 \times \text{max\_ep\_len}$ steps are collected before each PPO update.

\subsection{Loss Functions}
\label{sec:loss_functions}

Four distinct losses are minimized at each update cycle. They share the same rollout buffer but are optimized with respect to disjoint parameter groups, so no joint optimization is required.

\textbf{PPO Actor Loss $L^\text{PPO}(\theta)$.} Define the importance ratio $\rho_t(\theta) = \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t) / \pi_{\theta_\text{old}}(a_t \mid s_t)$ and let $\hat A_t$ denote the advantage estimate computed by Generalized Advantage Estimation (GAE) \cite{schulman2016gae} from the shaped reward $\bar r_t$. The clipped surrogate objective \cite{schulman2017ppo} is
\begin{equation}
L^\text{PPO}(\theta) \;=\;
-\,\mathbb{E}_t\!\left[
\min\!\Bigl(
\rho_t(\theta)\, \hat A_t,\,
\text{clip}\bigl(\rho_t(\theta),\, 1-\epsilon,\, 1+\epsilon\bigr)\, \hat A_t
\Bigr)\right].
\label{eq:ppo_loss}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Critic Loss $L^V(\mu)$.} The value network $V_\mu$ is fitted by mean-squared error against the GAE return target $\hat V_t$:
\begin{equation}
L^V(\mu) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[ \bigl( V_\mu(s_t) - \hat V_t \bigr)^2 \right].
\label{eq:critic_loss}
\end{equation}

\textbf{ICM Loss $L_\text{ICM}(\eta)$.} As specified in Equation~\eqref{eq:icm_loss}.

\textbf{Beta Loss $L_\beta(\psi)$.} As specified in Equation~\eqref{eq:beta_loss}.

The four losses correspond to four parameter groups: $\theta$ (actor), $\mu$ (critic), $\eta$ (ICM), and $\psi$ (Beta Network). Gradients flow through each loss with respect to its own parameter group only; in particular, the Beta Network is not updated through $L^\text{PPO}$ and the policy is not updated through $L_\beta$.

\subsection{Training Procedure}
\label{sec:training_procedure}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:care} specifies the complete CARE training loop. One iteration of the outer loop corresponds to a single PPO update cycle.

\begin{algorithm}[H]
\caption{CARE training loop with PPO and ICM.}
\label{alg:care}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Initialize policy $\theta$, critic $\mu$, ICM $\eta$, Beta Network $\psi$
\For{$\text{update} = 1, 2, \dots, U$}
    \State Collect $T$ on-policy transitions $\{\xi_t\}_{t=0}^{T-1}$ using $\pi_\theta$
    \State Compute raw intrinsic rewards $I_t$ via Equation~\eqref{eq:icm_raw}
    \State Standardize and rectify to obtain $I_t^+$ via Equation~\eqref{eq:icm_standardized}
    \State Compute discounted extrinsic returns $G_t^E$ via Equation~\eqref{eq:extrinsic_return}
    \State Update ICM parameters: $\eta \gets \eta - \eta_\text{lr}\, \nabla_\eta L_\text{ICM}(\eta)$
    \State Update Beta Network: $\psi \gets \psi - \psi_\text{lr}\, \nabla_\psi L_\beta(\psi)$
    \State Compute shaped rewards $\bar r_t = R_t^E + \alpha\, \beta_\psi(s_t)\, I_t^+$
    \State Compute GAE advantages $\hat A_t$ from $\{\bar r_t\}$ and $V_\mu$
    \For{$\text{epoch} = 1, 2, \dots, K$}
        \State Update actor: $\theta \gets \theta - \theta_\text{lr}\, \nabla_\theta L^\text{PPO}(\theta)$
        \State Update critic: $\mu \gets \mu - \mu_\text{lr}\, \nabla_\mu L^V(\mu)$
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

The algorithm preserves the standard PPO update structure (lines 11--14) and inserts only two additional optimization steps (lines 7--8) before the shaped reward is computed. This modularity is important: the same template applies if ICM is replaced by a count-based bonus or by RND, with only Equations~\eqref{eq:icm_raw}--\eqref{eq:icm_standardized} being substituted by the corresponding module-specific computation. Concrete numerical values for all hyperparameters and network architectures used in the experiments are listed in Appendix~\ref{app:implementation}.
